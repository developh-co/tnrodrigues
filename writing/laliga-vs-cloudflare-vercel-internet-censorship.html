When a Football League Starts Censoring the Internet

TECH, SOCIETY, OPINION

When a Football League Starts Censoring the Internet

Something strange is happening in Spain: a football league is deciding what parts of the internet people can and cannot access.
This isn’t an exaggeration — it’s real.

LaLiga, the organization behind Spanish professional football, is aggressively using court orders to block IPs linked to illegal streams of its matches. On the surface, it looks like an effort to protect broadcast rights. But in practice, it’s taking down thousands of legitimate websites, essential developer infrastructure, and digital businesses — all because they share the same IP addresses.

The Scale of the Block

In December 2024, a Barcelona court gave LaLiga legal authority to require ISPs like Movistar, Vodafone, and Orange to block IPs linked to unauthorized streams. That decision was upheld in March 2025, giving LaLiga the green light to enforce these blocks without distinguishing between legal and illegal services.

This means that one IP used by a pirate site might also be used by hundreds of other legitimate sites — hosted on platforms like Cloudflare, Vercel, GitHub Pages, or BunnyCDN.
When the IP is blocked, everything behind it disappears — with no warning, no transparency, and no due process.

This Isn’t Targeted Enforcement. It’s Mass Censorship.

Normally, ISPs can block individual domains using techniques like Server Name Indication (SNI) in the TLS handshake. This allows them to isolate traffic by hostname, without affecting other domains on the same IP.

That’s not what’s happening here.

Spanish ISPs are blocking entire IP addresses — ignoring SNI — and in doing so, they're pulling the plug on businesses, tools, and platforms that have nothing to do with piracy.

And this happens mostly during LaLiga matchdays — weekends and midweek games. The censorship follows the football calendar.

What’s at Stake?

This is more than a copyright issue. It’s a dangerous shift in who gets to shape the internet.

A private sports organization — with clear commercial interests — is now deciding which parts of the internet stay online.

We are trading network neutrality and open access for broad, blind shutdowns, enforced through court orders and executed by ISPs with zero granularity.

How the Platforms Respond

Vercel, for example, says it has a dedicated abuse team that promptly removes illegal content from its platform. They comply with takedown requests and work with authorities. Still, their IPs are being blocked.

“This isn’t a narrowly scoped takedown. It’s blanket censorship.”
— Malte Ubl, CTO at Vercel

Cloudflare, GitHub, BunnyCDN — all facing similar collateral damage. Millions of users are being affected by a system that’s designed to punish many for the actions of a few.

What We Stand For

At Developh, we believe in an internet that’s open, accessible, and technically sound.
Blocking entire chunks of infrastructure is not a modern solution to a digital problem.

There are smarter, more transparent ways to fight piracy — ways that don’t punish users, developers and startups along the way.

Defending streaming rights? Absolutely.
Breaking the internet to do it? No thanks.
